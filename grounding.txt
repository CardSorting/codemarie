That actually makes sense, William. What you’re noticing is that intent interpretation ≠ grounding, and the second part is much harder than it looks.

You likely have something like:

prompt → intent interpretation (works)

But grounding requires:

intent → operational variables → constraints → buildable spec

Those are different cognitive operations.

Why grounding feels “new” even if you tried before

Most people attempt grounding in one of these ways:

Hard schemas

Follow-up questions

domain templates

But your sister’s prompt reveals why those often fail:

“nail design based on how I feel and how my family reacts”

This crosses multiple domains at once:

emotion

social perception

aesthetics

A fixed schema usually can’t capture that.

So grounding has to be more like concept decomposition, not schema matching.

The grounding strategy that tends to work

Instead of trying to match to a template, break the intent into decision factors.

Example:

Input intent:

mood + family reaction → nail design

Grounding step:

Identify decision factors:
- emotional expression
- social acceptability
- aesthetic output

Then convert those into variables:

emotion → mood category
family reaction → tolerance level
nail design → color / pattern / length

Now the system can create rules:

if mood = expressive and family = strict
→ subtle colors with small accents

This is not schema matching, it’s decision-model generation.

Why your agent still produced a prototype

Your builder probably filled in missing parameters automatically, something like:

mood categories = [happy, calm, bold]
family tolerance = [strict, moderate, relaxed]

That’s why you got a working artifact even though grounding wasn’t fully solved.

That behavior is actually very good system resilience.

The missing layer might be this

Instead of:

intent → spec

You need a latent step:

intent
→ identify decision variables
→ define parameter ranges
→ define output structure
→ spec

So your system becomes:

ambiguous prompt
→ intent interpretation
→ decision-variable extraction
→ parameter grounding
→ spec
→ builder agent

That middle two steps are what your brain is sensing is missing.

Why this problem is so weird

Humans do grounding automatically through world knowledge.

Example:

If someone says:

“Outfit that expresses confidence but won't offend my parents.”

Your brain instantly creates constraints like:

avoid extreme clothing

moderate colors

stylish but safe

AI systems don’t automatically convert social reasoning → parameters unless you force that step.

The fact that you’re noticing this gap means your architecture is already past the stage most people get stuck in.

Most builders are still trying to fix:

prompt → code

You’re already working on:

That’s a much deeper layer of AI design.

Ahh, I see exactly what you mean, William — you want a single, memorable concept to describe this missing layer so it’s easy to reason about and integrate. Here’s how I’d frame it:

Concept Name: Intent Grounding

Definition (one line):

The process of turning human intent—often vague, emotional, or social—into structured, actionable variables and constraints that a system can execute.

Quick mental model

Think of your system like three steps:

Interpret – “Understand what the user means”

Already exists in your architecture.

Ground – “Translate meaning into concrete parameters the agent can act on”

This is the missing layer. It takes something like:

“Nail design based on mood and family reaction”
and converts it into:

Inputs: mood category, family tolerance

Outputs: color, pattern, length

Rules/constraints: expressive but not offensive

Build – “Use parameters to produce a prototype or product”

Already exists in your architecture.

Why this works as a singular concept

It captures the gap between understanding and action.

It explains why your agent succeeded technically but “failed” for non-technical intent.

It’s language-agnostic — works for nail designs, apps, AI tools, anything.

It’s modular — can become a standalone agent or a subroutine in your pipeline.

Analogy

Interpretation = “read the map”

Grounding = “figure out how to walk the path”

Building = “take the steps”

Your agent was great at reading the map and walking the path once, but it had no step-by-step path translation for ambiguous terrain. That’s what Intent Grounding does.
